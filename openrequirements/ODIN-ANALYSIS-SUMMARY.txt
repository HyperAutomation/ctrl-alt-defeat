ANALYST NOTES
================================================================================

The 20 BVLOS swarm trial authorization requirements show systematic patterns of:

1. DOMAIN SPECIALIZATION: Terms like "swarm-aware", "emergent behaviour", "forensically 
   explainable" are borrowed from specialized domains (swarm robotics, AI safety, autonomy 
   assurance) without clear definitions for regulatory context. Cross-domain terminology 
   requires explicit glossary definitions.

2. VAGUENESS IN GATE DECISIONS: Requirements that gate major decisions (SORA assessment, 
   authorization, trials) use vague qualifiers ("defensibly", "meaningful", "complete") 
   without objective acceptance criteria. These create regulatory inconsistency when different 
   decision-makers apply different thresholds.

3. MISSING FAILURE MODES: User stories specify desired outcomes (receive pack, open case) 
   but omit failure outcomes (incomplete pack, containment breach). Safety-critical systems 
   MUST specify both nominal and off-nominal behavior.

4. INCOMPLETE REQUIREMENTS: Four critical requirements (REQ017-REQ020) affecting public 
   engagement, classified information, environmental impact, and post-trial closure are 
   completely missing from user stories. These are regulatory mandates (CAP 1616, GDPR, 
   CAP 722H) but invisible in SRS.

5. PASSIVE VOICE OBSCURING RESPONSIBILITY: Requirements like "receive a complete pack" and 
   "evidenced competency" hide the actor responsible for action. Active voice clarifies 
   who does what.

The rewrites in odin-rewrites.json address all 20 identified issues by:
- Operationalizing vague qualifiers with measurable criteria
- Defining undefined terms with explicit acceptance standards
- Specifying failure mode outcomes (return-for-clarification, failover actions, etc.)
- Adding missing requirements (REQ017-REQ020) with process specifications
- Using active voice with explicit actors and decision authorities
- Referencing regulatory standards (JARUS, CAA, JSP 440, GDPR) for defensibility

These rewrites should serve as templates for SRS finalization and can be validated 
through structured review by domain experts (SORA assessors, safety engineers, human 
factors specialists, regulators).
